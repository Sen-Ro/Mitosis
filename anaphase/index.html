Anaphase
Anaphase is the third stage of mitosis, where the sister chromatids are separated and pulled toward opposite poles of the cell.
  1. Sister Chromatids Separate
    • The proteins holding the sister chromatids together are broken down.
    • Each chromatid becomes an individual chromosome.
  2. Chromosomes Move to Opposite Poles
    • Spindle fibers shorten, pulling the chromosomes to opposite sides of the cell.
    • This ensures that each daughter cell receives an identical set of chromosomes.
  3. Cell Elongates
    • The non-attached spindle fibers push against each other, stretching the cell.
    • This elongation helps prepare the cell for division.
  4. Ensuring Equal Chromosome Distribution
    • Anaphase is a rapid phase, ensuring the accurate and equal division of genetic material.
    • Errors in this phase can lead to conditions such as aneuploidy (unequal chromosome numbers).
